# Writing a Bibliography for a SoCG / SODA-Style Paper

This document is a practical, opinionated guide to writing the
bibliography (`References` section) of a paper aimed at **SoCG**
(Symposium on Computational Geometry, published in LIPIcs by Dagstuhl)
or **SODA** (ACM-SIAM Symposium on Discrete Algorithms, published by
SIAM). Most other theoretical-CS / theoretical-CG venues — STOC, FOCS,
ESA, ISAAC, WADS, SoCG, SODA, ICALP, PODC, DISC, and the journals
*Algorithmica*, *Discrete & Computational Geometry*, *SIAM Journal on
Computing*, *Journal of the ACM*, *Computational Geometry: Theory and
Applications*, *Journal of Discrete Algorithms*, *International Journal
of Computational Geometry & Applications* — follow essentially the
same conventions, with small differences described below.

The guide is organized so that you can copy-paste and adapt: every
rule is followed by a concrete, correctly-typed example. It also
includes a worked example based on Paz Carmi's most-cited and most
recent papers, since the spanner / computational geometry community
that this paper targets is exactly his community.

---

## 1. The two house styles you actually need to know

Almost every CG / discrete-algorithms paper you will read or write is
formatted in one of these two styles:

### 1a. LIPIcs / Dagstuhl (SoCG, ESA, ICALP, ITCS, MFCS, STACS, FSTTCS, ISAAC[*], SAT, CCC, DISC[*], FUN, ITP, ...)

- Document class: `lipics-v2021.cls` (currently the standard).
- BibTeX style: **`plainurl`** (mandatory) — no author-year, no `natbib`.
- Citations are numeric: `[12]` or `[3, 7, 12]`.
- The bibliography is generated by **BibTeX from a `.bib` file**;
  the camera-ready guidelines explicitly forbid `\begin{thebibliography}`
  with hand-written `\bibitem` entries and explicitly forbid `natbib`.
- DOIs are required when available; arXiv IDs are encouraged.
- Bibliography entries are typically copy-pasted from
  [DBLP](https://dblp.org)'s "BibTeX" export with minor cleanup.

[*] ISAAC and DISC have alternated between LIPIcs and Springer LNCS
over the years; check the year you are submitting to.

### 1b. SIAM / SODA (and most SIAM journals: SICOMP, SIDMA, SIMAX, SIIMS, ...)

- Document class: `siamart.cls` / `soda.cls` for proceedings.
- BibTeX style: **`siamplain.bst`** (or `siamonline.bst`).
- Author names are typeset in **\sc small-caps** by the `.bst`.
- Article titles are **lowercase except first word and proper nouns**;
  book titles are **Title Case**; titles are **italicized** by the `.bst`.
- Journal names use **Mathematical Reviews abbreviations**
  (e.g. *SIAM J. Comput.*, *Discrete Comput. Geom.*, *Algorithmica*).
- Citations are numeric and ordered alphabetically by author surname.

ACM venues outside of SIAM (STOC, PODC, SoCG-pre-2015, the older ACM
Trans.\ family) historically used `acmart` with the `acmnumeric`
bibstyle; the conventions are nearly identical to LIPIcs / SIAM apart
from cosmetic punctuation.

### 1c. The "I'll just hand-roll `\begin{thebibliography}`" style

This is what `dgf2.tex` in this repo currently uses, and it is fine
for an arXiv preprint or an internal draft. **Do not submit it like
that to SoCG or SODA.** Both venues expect a real `.bib` file run
through their `.bst`. Section 6 below shows how to convert a
hand-rolled bibliography into a proper `.bib` file.

## 2. The Five Iron Rules

These are the rules that 95% of SoCG/SODA papers follow. If you
internalize only this section you will already produce a bibliography
that looks "native" to the community.

1. **One BibTeX entry per logical work, not per appearance.** A paper
   that appeared in conference C and was then extended in journal J
   gets *one* entry that lists **both** the conference version and the
   journal version. The journal venue is primary; the conference
   pointer is given in a `note` field. Example below.

2. **Cite the journal version when one exists.** Conferences in this
   community routinely accept extended abstracts; the journal version
   has the proofs and is the *citable* version. If only a conference
   version exists, that is what you cite.

3. **Authors' first names are abbreviated to initials, separated by
   thin spaces.** Pierre Bose, Paz Carmi, ... → `P.~Bose, P.~Carmi, ...`
   The `~` is a non-breaking space and is essential. (LIPIcs `plainurl`
   does this for you if you put the full name in the `.bib` file.)

4. **Book and journal titles are italicized; paper titles are
   upright.** In hand-rolled style this is `\emph{...}` for the venue.
   In BibTeX with `plainurl` / `siamplain.bst` it is automatic — just
   put the unmodified title in the `title` field.

5. **Always include a stable identifier.** DOI for journal papers and
   modern conference papers; arXiv ID for preprints; URL only as a
   last resort. Never cite a personal homepage PDF without an arXiv
   mirror.

## 3. Anatomy of every entry type, with worked examples

The examples below use **two parallel forms**:

- **BibTeX form** — what you put in `references.bib`. This is what
  you should be writing.
- **Rendered form** — roughly what the reader sees after BibTeX runs
  with `plainurl` (LIPIcs) or `siamplain` (SODA). I show the LIPIcs
  rendering; SIAM is the same content with small-caps authors and
  abbreviated journal names.

All examples are real papers from the spanner / computational
geometry literature, so you can drop them straight into your `.bib`
file.

### 3.1 Journal article — **the most common case**

```bibtex
@article{BCFMS10,
  author    = {Prosenjit Bose and Paz Carmi and Mohammad Farshi and
               Anil Maheshwari and Michiel Smid},
  title     = {Computing the Greedy Spanner in Near-Quadratic Time},
  journal   = {Algorithmica},
  volume    = {58},
  number    = {3},
  pages     = {711--729},
  year      = {2010},
  doi       = {10.1007/s00453-009-9293-4},
}
```

Renders as:

> [12] P. Bose, P. Carmi, M. Farshi, A. Maheshwari, and M. Smid.
> Computing the greedy spanner in near-quadratic time.
> *Algorithmica*, 58(3):711–729, 2010. doi:10.1007/s00453-009-9293-4.

Notes:

- **Page range uses `--`** (en-dash), never `-`.
- **Author list separator** is `and`, not commas. BibTeX inserts the
  Oxford comma and "and" for you.
- The `volume(number)` notation `58(3)` is the SIAM/Algorithmica
  default; LIPIcs `plainurl` gives the same.
- Do **not** put `\emph{}` or `\textit{}` in any field — the `.bst`
  applies the formatting.

### 3.2 Conference paper that has *also* appeared as a journal paper

This is the case that trips up most students. The standard CG
community convention is **one entry, journal-primary, conference in
note**:

```bibtex
@article{FS20,
  author  = {Arnold Filtser and Shay Solomon},
  title   = {The Greedy Spanner is Existentially Optimal},
  journal = {SIAM Journal on Computing},
  volume  = {49},
  number  = {2},
  pages   = {429--447},
  year    = {2020},
  doi     = {10.1137/18M1210678},
  note    = {Preliminary version in \emph{PODC 2016}, pages 9--17.},
}
```

Renders as:

> [21] A. Filtser and S. Solomon. The greedy spanner is existentially
> optimal. *SIAM Journal on Computing*, 49(2):429–447, 2020.
> Preliminary version in *PODC 2016*, pages 9–17.
> doi:10.1137/18M1210678.

Why one entry, not two: a citation `\cite{FS20}` should resolve to a
single bibliography number that the reader can look up once and find
both the rigorous journal version and the historical conference
version. Two separate entries force the reader to follow two
pointers and pretend they are the same result.

### 3.3 Conference-only paper

```bibtex
@inproceedings{BLW19,
  author    = {Glencora Borradaile and Hung Le and Christian Wulff-Nilsen},
  title     = {Greedy Spanners are Optimal in Doubling Metrics},
  booktitle = {Proceedings of the 30th Annual ACM-SIAM Symposium on
               Discrete Algorithms (SODA 2019)},
  pages     = {2371--2379},
  year      = {2019},
  doi       = {10.1137/1.9781611975482.145},
}
```

Renders as:

> [9] G. Borradaile, H. Le, and C. Wulff-Nilsen. Greedy spanners are
> optimal in doubling metrics. In *Proc.\ SODA 2019*, pages 2371–2379.
> doi:10.1137/1.9781611975482.145.

For LIPIcs venues a much shorter `booktitle` is acceptable
(`booktitle = {Proceedings of the 35th International Symposium on
Computational Geometry (SoCG 2019)}`) — DBLP gives you the right
canonical string for free.

### 3.4 Book

```bibtex
@book{NS07,
  author    = {Giri Narasimhan and Michiel Smid},
  title     = {Geometric Spanner Networks},
  publisher = {Cambridge University Press},
  year      = {2007},
  doi       = {10.1017/CBO9780511546884},
}
```

Renders as:

> [33] G. Narasimhan and M. Smid. *Geometric Spanner Networks*.
> Cambridge University Press, 2007. doi:10.1017/CBO9780511546884.

For a chapter inside an edited book use `@incollection`.

### 3.5 Thesis

```bibtex
@phdthesis{Carmi08,
  author  = {Paz Carmi},
  title   = {Geometric and Combinatorial Optimization Problems},
  school  = {Ben-Gurion University of the Negev},
  year    = {2008},
}
```

### 3.6 arXiv-only / preprint / unpublished manuscript

```bibtex
@misc{BazCM21,
  author       = {Sina Bazarghani and Paz Carmi and Pat Morin},
  title        = {Grids have Unbounded Anagram-Free Chromatic Number},
  year         = {2021},
  eprint       = {2105.01916},
  archivePrefix= {arXiv},
  primaryClass = {math.CO},
  note         = {Preprint, arXiv:2105.01916},
}
```

If/when the paper is later published, replace the `@misc` with an
`@article` or `@inproceedings` — but keep the BibTeX key the same so
your `\cite{BazCM21}` calls do not break.

### 3.7 Technical report

```bibtex
@techreport{Yao82-TR,
  author      = {Andrew Chi-Chih Yao},
  title       = {On Constructing Minimum Spanning Trees in $k$-Dimensional Spaces},
  institution = {Stanford University},
  number      = {STAN-CS-77-642},
  year        = {1977},
}
```

In practice, prefer the journal form (`@article`) if it exists.

### 3.8 Software / dataset

This is increasingly accepted at SoCG (LIPIcs supports a `\supplement`
metadata field) and at experimental ALENEX / ESA Track B papers:

```bibtex
@misc{SpannerCompare25,
  author = {Noam Horovitz},
  title  = {{SpannerComparison}: Julia implementations of greedy and
            descending-greedy filter spanners},
  year   = {2025},
  url    = {https://github.com/.../SpannerComparison},
  note   = {Software, version 1.0.0},
}
```

## 4. The DBLP workflow (the only sane way)

Almost every paper in this community is indexed by **DBLP**
([https://dblp.org](https://dblp.org)). The recommended workflow is:

1. Search the paper on DBLP.
2. Click the small "export record" icon next to the entry.
3. Choose **BibTeX (condensed)** — *not* "BibTeX (with crossref)" and
   *not* "BibTeX (standard)". Condensed gives you a single
   self-contained entry without the `crossref` indirection that the
   sample LIPIcs `.bib` file showed in §3.
4. Paste it into your `.bib` file.
5. **Rename the BibTeX key.** DBLP keys look like
   `DBLP:journals/algorithmica/BoseCFMS10`. Replace with a short
   author-year mnemonic (`BCFMS10`, `Filt­SolGreedyOpt`, etc.) that you
   can actually remember when typing `\cite{...}`.
6. **Optionally strip the housekeeping fields.** `timestamp`,
   `biburl`, `bibsource` are harmless but noisy; remove them if you
   like a clean `.bib` file.
7. Add a `note` field if the paper has a journal version that DBLP
   gave you the conference version of, or vice versa.

Why DBLP and not Google Scholar: DBLP is curated, has stable
canonical author names (no "P. Carmi" vs "P Carmi" vs "Paz Carmi"
chaos), and the BibTeX it gives you is correct. Google Scholar
exports are notoriously wrong about pages, volume numbers, journal
names, and author-name diacritics.

For papers from before ~1995, or for some workshop papers, DBLP can
be incomplete. In that case use the **publisher** page (Springer,
ACM DL, SIAM, ScienceDirect) for the canonical metadata, then write
the BibTeX entry by hand.

## 5. In-text citation style

### 5.1 LIPIcs / SoCG / ESA / ICALP

- Default is plain numeric: `\cite{BCFMS10}` → `[12]`.
- Multiple cites group: `\cite{Cai94, Kortsarz01, SZ04}` → `[5, 14, 22]`.
- For "see X" or "X showed", put the author name in text and the
  citation in brackets:
  > Bose, Carmi, Farshi, Maheshwari, and Smid~\cite{BCFMS10} showed
  > that the greedy spanner can be computed in $O(n^2 \log n)$ time.
  Note the **`~`** between the names and `\cite` — never break a
  citation onto a new line away from the surrounding text.
- For a result attributed to multiple papers, use a single grouped
  cite, listed in chronological order:
  > Several near-linear-space variants are known
  > \cite{ABBB15, BCFMS10, GLN02}.

### 5.2 SODA / SIAM

Same as LIPIcs in the body. SIAM additionally allows
"author~[ref]" with a tilde for non-breaking spacing. Do **not** use
the `\citet` / `\citep` macros from `natbib` — they don't render
correctly with `siamplain.bst`.

### 5.3 Author-name conventions in text

- **First mention** in the body: list all authors if there are at
  most three; otherwise list first author followed by *et al.*
  > Bose and Carmi~\cite{...}
  > Bose, Carmi, and Smid~\cite{...}
  > Bose et~al.~\cite{...}     % four or more authors
- **Subsequent mentions:** *et al.* is acceptable from the second
  mention onward even for two-author papers, but most CG papers keep
  full names throughout for two- and three-author teams.
- Diacritics: keep them. `Alth\"ofer`, `Ros\'en`, `Dujmovi\'c` —
  these names are part of the author's identity and SoCG/SODA reviewers
  notice when they're missing. UTF-8 in the `.bib` works in modern
  TeX Live but the safe form is the LaTeX-escaped one above.

## 6. Converting `dgf2.tex`'s hand-rolled bibliography to a real `.bib`

`dgf2.tex` currently has a `\begin{thebibliography}{99}` block with
`\bibitem{...}` entries. This is fine for the working draft and for
arXiv, but for camera-ready SoCG / SODA / journal submission you
should:

1. Create a sibling file `references.bib` next to `dgf2.tex`.
2. For each existing `\bibitem{Key}` write a real BibTeX entry whose
   key is the same `Key`. (DBLP-export the paper, rename the DBLP key
   to `Key`.) This means **no `\cite{...}` call in the body has to
   change** — a critical property when you're under deadline.
3. Replace the entire `\begin{thebibliography}...\end{thebibliography}`
   block with:

   ```latex
   \bibliographystyle{plainurl}    % LIPIcs / SoCG
   % \bibliographystyle{siamplain} % SIAM / SODA
   \bibliography{references}
   ```

4. Run `pdflatex dgf2 && bibtex dgf2 && pdflatex dgf2 && pdflatex dgf2`
   and check the `.blg` log for warnings (missing fields, undefined
   crossrefs, etc.).

## 8. Common bibliography mistakes (and how reviewers react to them)

Reviewers in this community are notoriously picky about
bibliographies. The following mistakes will not get your paper
rejected, but they *will* get a paragraph in the review that starts
with "the bibliography is sloppy" — which is something you want to
avoid.

| Mistake | Right way |
|---|---|
| `pages = {711-729}` (hyphen) | `pages = {711--729}` (en-dash) |
| `journal = {SIAM J. on Computing}` invented abbrev. | Use the journal's official MR-style abbreviation; or spell out in full. Be consistent. |
| Two entries for conference + journal version | One entry, journal-primary, conference in `note`. |
| `author = {P. Bose, P. Carmi, M. Smid}` (commas) | `author = {Prosenjit Bose and Paz Carmi and Michiel Smid}` (`and` separated, full names). BibTeX will abbreviate. |
| `\cite[BCFMS10]` — missing space | `Bose et~al.~\cite{BCFMS10}` (tilde, not space) |
| Citing arXiv when a journal version exists | Always prefer the journal version. |
| `\textit{Algorithmica}` inside the `journal` field | Never style inside the field; let the `.bst` decide. |
| Missing DOI / arXiv ID | Add them. LIPIcs's `plainurl` displays them as clickable links. |
| Citing a personal-website PDF | Find the official venue or arXiv mirror. Personal-PDF citations rot. |
| `Alth\"ofer` written as `Althoefer` | Keep diacritics; this is the author's name. |
| Two entries that differ only in capitalization or whitespace of the BibTeX key | Each work appears exactly once with one canonical key. |
| Inconsistent author ordering ("Carmi, P." vs "P.\ Carmi") | Pick one and let the `.bst` enforce it (use BibTeX, not hand-rolled). |
| Citing a textbook for a result that has a primary source | Cite the primary source; cite the textbook only as "see also \cite{NS07}". |

## 9. What "completeness" means in a SoCG/SODA bibliography

A SoCG/SODA bibliography is generally **dense and exhaustive within
its narrow topic**, not encyclopedic. The typical rule of thumb:

- **Cite every paper you actually use.** If a result, a definition,
  or even a non-trivial proof technique comes from another paper,
  cite it at the point of use.
- **Cite every paper a knowledgeable reader would expect to see.**
  In a paper on greedy spanners this means at minimum:
  ADDJS93, KG92, CDNS95, GLN02, NS07, BCFMS10, ABBB15, FS20, LS22,
  BLW19. Missing any of these is a flag.
- **Do not pad.** Citing a textbook for the definition of a "graph"
  is unnecessary. Citing two surveys when one is enough is
  unnecessary.
- **Survey papers and textbooks are cited once, at the start of the
  related-work section, with a "for a comprehensive survey see ..."
  pointer.** They are not re-cited for every standard fact.
- **A SoCG paper has 25–50 references.** A SODA paper has 30–70. A
  short workshop paper has 10–20. A survey or journal version has
  60–150. If your count is wildly outside these ranges, ask a senior
  author whether the paper is over- or under-grounded.

---

## 10. The minimum self-test before submission

Before you send the paper, run this 90-second checklist:

1. `bibtex dgf2` produces **zero warnings** (or only known-harmless
   ones).
2. Every `\cite{Key}` resolves — search the `.aux` for `Citation
   undefined`.
3. No entry in `references.bib` is **unused** — search the `.blg`
   for "unused entry"; remove or comment them out.
4. Every entry has either a DOI, an arXiv ID, or a stable URL.
5. Every conference paper that has a journal version cites the
   journal version (DBLP makes this easy: search the conference
   entry, look at the linked "journal version" pointer).
6. Author names are consistent: open `references.bib` and grep for
   each author's surname; make sure their initials and accents are
   the same in every entry.
7. The bibliography compiles with both `plainurl` (for the LIPIcs
   submission) and `siamplain` (for the SODA submission), or
   whichever target you actually have. They should produce different
   typography but identical content.
8. Cross-check three random entries against DBLP / the publisher
   page. If any of the three has wrong pages, wrong year, or a
   missing co-author, audit the rest.

## 11. Further reading (canonical references for the bibliography style itself)

- Dagstuhl Publishing. *LIPIcs Authors' Guidelines* (latest version).
  GitHub: `dagstuhl-publishing/styles`, folder `LIPIcs/authors/`.
  This is the authoritative source for the SoCG / ESA / ICALP /
  ITCS bibliography format.
- SIAM. *SIAM LaTeX Style Manual.* See `siamplain.bst` and the
  accompanying *Guide to Using SIAM's LaTeX Style*. This is the
  authoritative source for SODA, SICOMP, and SIDMA.
- DBLP — `https://dblp.org`. The single best source of correct
  BibTeX entries for the entire computer science theory literature.
- Narasimhan, G. and Smid, M. *Geometric Spanner Networks.*
  Cambridge University Press, 2007. Chapters 6, 8, and 14 are the
  closest thing the spanner community has to a canonical citation
  graph; using their reference list as a reality-check on your own
  is a good final-pass exercise.
